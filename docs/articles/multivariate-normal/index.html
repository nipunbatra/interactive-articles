Seeing the Multivariate Normal

Start with a bell curve on a line. Stretch it into an elliptical cloud on a plane. Then slice that cloud to see where marginals and conditionals come from — and why the Gaussian is the first distribution most practitioners reach for.

I. The bell curve you already know

A normal distribution in one dimension is a statement about where points tend to land on a number line. There is a center, called μ, and a width, called σ. Points close to the center are common. Points far from it are exponentially rare. That single sentence is the whole idea.

The density function looks complicated but says something simple: the farther you get from the mean, the less likely you are to land there, and the rate of that drop-off is controlled entirely by the standard deviation.

\[ f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \]

Pull μ left or right and the whole curve slides. Increase σ and the curve spreads out: the peak drops because the same total probability must cover a wider range. The shaded region is not “height”; it is area under the curve. That area is the probability that a draw from the distribution lands inside the chosen interval.

This is where people often get tripped up. The curve is not a histogram of observed counts. It is an idealized density. The only thing with probabilistic meaning is area, not the height at one exact point.

A good mental picture: exam scores around a class average, or measurement error around a sensor’s true reading. The mean tells you the center of the story; the standard deviation tells you how noisy the story is.

The standardized coordinate z = (x − μ) / σ removes the units and tells you how surprising a point is relative to the bell. When you center the interval around the mean, one, two, and three standard deviations collect about 68%, 95%, and 99.7% of the mass. Most of the later formulas in this article are just higher-dimensional versions of the same “recenter, then rescale” idea.

II. From a curve to a cloud

In two dimensions the normal distribution becomes a hill over the plane. Seen from above, equal-density contours look like ellipses. Their tilt and stretch come from the covariance matrix.

The conceptual jump from 1D to 2D is smaller than it looks. The mean becomes a point instead of a number. The variance becomes a 2×2 matrix instead of a single number. That matrix does two things: it sets the width along each axis, and it encodes whether the two variables tend to move together.

\[ \begin{bmatrix} X \\ Y \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mu_x \\ \mu_y \end{bmatrix},\; \Sigma \right), \qquad \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x^2 & \rho\,\sigma_x\sigma_y \\ \rho\,\sigma_x\sigma_y & \sigma_y^2 \end{bmatrix} \]

If ρ = 0, the cloud is axis-aligned: knowing x tells you nothing about y. As |ρ| grows, the cloud leans. Positive correlation tilts upward — large values of one variable tend to arrive with large values of the other. Negative correlation tilts downward.

The nested ellipses below are contour lines: each collects points with the same Mahalanobis distance from the mean. The dots are random draws from the current Gaussian. A practical way to read the covariance matrix: the diagonal terms set axis spread, the off-diagonal term controls tilt.

Another way to say the same thing: the covariance matrix chooses the shape of the ellipse that best summarizes uncertainty. Round means all directions are equally uncertain. Long and thin means one direction is much less certain than another. Tilt means the variables do not vary independently.

Where this shape shows up

Height and weight. Taller people tend to weigh more, so the cloud leans upward. Speed and travel time. Faster trips take less time, so the cloud leans downward. GPS on a road. The position is much more uncertain along the road than across it, producing a thin ridge. Two measurements from one drifting machine. If the machine drifts, both readings drift together and the cloud becomes narrow along one preferred direction. The distribution does not care what the axes mean. What changes is the story you map onto the same geometry.

III. Sampling: the cloud becomes points

A Gaussian is not just a contour plot. It is a recipe for generating actual points. Start with a standard normal — a circular cloud centered at the origin — then stretch, rotate, and shift it so it picks up the mean and covariance you want.

\[ z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I), \qquad x = \mu + L\,z, \qquad LL^\top = \Sigma \]

The matrix L is the Cholesky factor of the covariance. It does the stretching and rotating. This matters because it turns a fancy-looking model into a very concrete procedure: draw simple random numbers, then apply a linear transformation. The multivariate normal is not mysterious once you see it as a transformed standard normal.

The figure below uses the same parameters from the 2D controls above. Increase the sample size and the cloud starts to look more like the target ellipse. Small samples wobble — they can look lopsided, off-center, or more correlated than the true model. That is not a bug. It is what sampling noise looks like.

Sample estimates wobble less as n grows. Finite samples are noisy. Larger samples settle down around the true mean and covariance.

IV. Fitting a Gaussian to data

Once you have data, the Gaussian can be fit with two summaries: the average location and the average spread around that location. These are the maximum-likelihood estimates.

\[ \hat{\mu} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i, \qquad \hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \hat{\mu})(x_i - \hat{\mu})^\top \]

Intuitively: the mean asks where is the center of the cloud? The covariance asks once I recenter the cloud, how does it spread out, and in which directions does it spread together? Those two summaries are enough to reconstruct the full multivariate normal. Nothing is hidden behind a magical optimizer.

V. Marginals: what one axis sees

A marginal distribution answers the simplest possible question: if I forget one of the two coordinates entirely, what does the remaining coordinate look like? For a Gaussian, the answer is still Gaussian, and you read the parameters straight off the diagonal of the covariance matrix.

\[ f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_x,\;\sigma_x^2) \]

This is the key surprise: correlation does not change the marginal widths. You can slide ρ all the way from −0.95 to +0.95 and the teal marginal curves below stay exactly the same width. The joint cloud rotates, but the shadows it casts onto each axis do not.

The figure below shows the joint distribution in the center, with the X marginal projected above and the Y marginal projected to the right. Change the parameters in Section II and these plots update together.

This is why marginals are the cleanest place to see what the variances alone are doing. The marginal “forgets” the relationship between x and y. It only remembers each axis’s own spread. To learn something about relationships, you need conditionals.

VI. Conditionals: what one observation tells you

A conditional distribution answers a sharper question: if I observe that X = x0, what does the remaining uncertainty about Y look like? For a Gaussian, the answer is again Gaussian, but now the mean slides and the variance shrinks.

\[ Y \mid X = x_0 \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\Big( \mu_y + \rho\,\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x}(x_0 - \mu_x),\; (1-\rho^2)\,\sigma_y^2 \Big) \]

Two things happen. First, the conditional mean moves: it slides along the tilted cloud toward wherever the observation predicts. Second, the conditional variance shrinks by the factor (1 − ρ²). Stronger correlation means a sharper conditional — observing one coordinate gives you more information about the other.

The figure below adds two slice lines to the joint plot. Drag them (or use the sliders) to see the conditional distributions appear. The gold curve on top shows X | Y = y0. The coral curve on the right shows Y | X = x0. Compare each to its teal marginal: the conditional is always narrower.

This is the payoff of the whole article. Up to now, the Gaussian has been a static object: a curve, then a cloud, then a sampling recipe, then a fitted model. Conditionals are where the object starts answering questions. What changes if I observe one coordinate? The mean slides. The spread shrinks. And the result is still Gaussian.

That closure property — Gaussian in, Gaussian out — is why this family dominates so many workflows, from Kalman filters to Bayesian regression. The algebra stays manageable and the picture stays readable, no matter how many times you condition.

VII. What to remember

The 1D base case
A Gaussian is a center and a width. Probability lives in area, not in the height at a single point.
The 2D upgrade
The mean becomes a point and the covariance matrix becomes an ellipse. Diagonal entries set axis spread; off-diagonal entries create tilt.
Sampling is concrete
To draw from a multivariate normal, transform a standard normal vector. The cloud is a recipe, not just a picture.
Learning is simple
Average the points for the mean. Average the centered outer products for the covariance. The difficulty is usually small data, not complicated formulas.
Marginals forget
Integrate out one axis and you keep only that axis’s own mean and variance. Correlation changes the joint cloud, not the marginal widths.
Conditionals focus
Observe one coordinate and the other stays Gaussian but becomes narrower. Stronger correlation means sharper focus.

If you remember one picture, remember this: a multivariate normal is a smooth cloud with a center, a preferred spread, and a tilt. Marginals look at the cloud from the side. Conditionals cut through it and become narrower when the variables agree.

If you remember one workflow, remember this: write down a mean and covariance, sample points, estimate the mean and covariance back from those points, then ask marginal and conditional questions. That loop is the full story in miniature.
