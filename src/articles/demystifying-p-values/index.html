Step 2

The "What If" Game (The Null Hypothesis)

In statistics, to know if our treatment actually worked, we must play the ultimate skeptic. We ask: What if the treatment did absolutely nothing at all?

This skeptical stance is called the Null Hypothesis. The word "null" simply means "zero" or "none" — as in, there is zero real difference between the two groups, and the treatment had no effect.

If the Null Hypothesis is true, then a subject's score wouldn't depend on which group they were assigned to. They would have gotten that exact same score regardless! The gap we observed between Group A and Group B would just be a random coincidence of how we split the 10 subjects on day one.

What does this mean?

  • p stands for probability.
  • It tells us: Under the assumption that there is NO real difference between the groups (the Null Hypothesis), what is the probability of seeing a gap this large (or larger) just by random chance?
  • A small p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests that the observed gap is very unlikely to be a random fluke, leading us to reject the Null Hypothesis and believe the treatment actually had an effect.
  • A large p-value means this kind of gap happens all the time by chance, so we can't confidently say the treatment did anything. We fail to reject the Null Hypothesis.
  • Crucial Warning: A p-value is not the probability that the treatment worked, nor is it the probability that the Null Hypothesis is true or false. It is simply the probability of getting this specific data if the Null Hypothesis were true.
Bonus

How does this fit into standard statistical tests?

What you just performed is called a Permutation Test (or Randomization Test). It is incredibly powerful because it is an exact test—it doesn't make assumptions about the shape of your data (like assuming a Bell Curve).

In practice, do statisticians calculate every single split?

Not always! For 10 items (5 vs 5), there are only 252 combinations. But for just 40 items (20 vs 20), there are over 137 billion combinations! Calculating every single one would take too long, even for modern computers. So, statisticians use two main shortcuts to calculate p-values:

  • Shortcut 1: Math Formulas (Parametric Tests) Before computers, statisticians invented clever mathematical formulas to estimate p-values. You might have heard of tests like the Student's t-test (comparing two groups, like we did), ANOVA (comparing three or more groups), or Chi-Square (comparing categories). These tests assume the data follows a smooth, predictable shape—like a Bell Curve. Instead of counting combinations, they use calculus to find the area under the curve to estimate the p-value.
  • Shortcut 2: Random Simulations (Monte Carlo) Today, instead of relying on complex math or strict assumptions, we can just use computers to do exactly what you did in Step 3! We don't need to calculate all 137 billion splits. We can just randomly shuffle the data 10,000 times. The p-value from 10,000 random shuffles is almost identical to the exact p-value, keeping the math simple, intuitive, and hands-on.
