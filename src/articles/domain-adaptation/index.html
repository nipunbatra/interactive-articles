Domain Shift, Adapted

The training distribution and deployment distribution rarely match. Slide the drift; watch the classifier collapse; align the source and target features with CORAL; recover accuracy without target labels. Then flag the truly out-of-distribution points with an energy score.

~16 min Distribution Shift · OOD · Adaptation
Prelude

The cross-city sensor problem

You train an air-quality model on Delhi sensor data; deploy it in Bengaluru. Same physical phenomenon, different calibration, different humidity, different wind. The classifier looks great on the source distribution and painfully wrong on the target. Domain adaptation is the family of techniques that let you do something about it — ideally without labelling the target.

  • Domain adaptation: labelled source + unlabelled target. Goal: a model that works on target.
  • Domain generalisation: multiple labelled source domains, no target during training. Goal: a model that works on an unseen target.
  • OOD detection: flag inputs that are too far from any training distribution to trust the prediction.

Step 2

CORAL — match second-order statistics

CORAL (Sun & Saenko, 2016): align the covariance of source features to the target's. Compute $C_S$ and $C_T$, whiten the source by $C_S^{-1/2}$, then re-colour by $C_T^{1/2}$. The classifier sees a source whose covariance matches the target.

Other heavy-hitters: DANN (domain-adversarial NN, gradient reversal so features are domain-indistinguishable), MMD (match feature means with a kernelised test), BatchNorm statistics adaptation (often shockingly strong with one line of code: recompute BN running stats on target). For tabular / sensor data, CORAL is usually a strong, training-free first try.

Step 3

OOD detection — when adaptation isn't enough

Adaptation closes the gap when source and target overlap. Some inputs at deployment are truly out of distribution — a satellite tile under a snowstorm when you trained on cloudless tiles. Flag them with an energy score:

The lower the negative log-sum-exp of logits, the further the input is from the training manifold. Threshold the score on a held-out validation set; everything below the threshold gets flagged. The ROC curve in the demo above shows separability between target-domain points and deliberately-distant noise points.

Step 4

Where this lives in your lab

  • Cross-city air-quality models. Train on AirDelhi mobile sensors; CORAL-align to a new city's distribution before deploying. Often recovers most of the source-domain accuracy with no fresh labelling.
  • Seasonal drift. Same city, different month. Re-fit BN running stats and add an OOD detector for monsoon-season frames.
  • Satellite domain transfer. Sentinel-2 L1C vs L2A; different sun angles; different sensor years. Test-time CORAL on the encoder features keeps your kiln detector working.
  • Multi-site clinical (ApneaEye). Each site is its own domain. Domain generalisation (training with several labelled sites + IRM-style penalty) gives a model that holds up at the next site.
Final takeaway. Distribution shift is the single most likely reason your deployed model is worse than your test number suggested. A 30-minute CORAL retrofit + OOD detector should be standard before any deployment.
