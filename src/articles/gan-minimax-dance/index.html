GAN Minimax Dance

GAN training is two networks chasing each other. The generator tries to fool the discriminator; the discriminator tries to catch the generator. At the Nash equilibrium, the generator matches the data distribution and the discriminator's output is 0.5 everywhere. Scrub the training-step slider and watch it happen.

~6 minDeep Learning · GAN · Generative

The real data (shown in sage) is a two-mode Gaussian — a bimodal distribution the generator has to discover. The generator's distribution starts as pure Gaussian noise at the origin. The discriminator D(x) starts confused. As training proceeds, the generator's distribution glides onto the real one, and D(x) flattens toward 0.5 everywhere — unable to tell real from fake.

Reading the picture

Step 0: generator is centered at 0, D is a sigmoid between the two modes. D is strong; G has no idea.

Step 200: generator has started to spread out; D's decision boundary is warping to chase the new fake locations. Losses oscillate.

Step 1000: generator's density matches real almost exactly. D(x) has collapsed to ~0.5 everywhere. That's the Nash equilibrium: the generator is indistinguishable from data, and the discriminator gives up.

In practice. Real GAN training is far messier — D often gets too strong too fast, G suffers mode collapse, losses are barely interpretable. This demo is the theoretical picture. WGAN, DCGAN guidelines, and spectral norm are all about making real GANs behave closer to this ideal trajectory.

The math it animates

minG maxD   Ex∼p[log D(x)] + Ez∼N[log(1−D(G(z)))]

At the optimum, the discriminator's best response to any fixed generator is D*(x) = p(x) / (p(x) + p_G(x)). When p_G = p, this gives D* = 1/2 everywhere — the flat line you see at convergence.
