Image Segmentation, Pixel by Pixel

Classification labels the image. Detection labels boxes. Segmentation labels every pixel. That last step forces every idea to get more precise: what's a correct answer, how do you measure overlap, and how do you even draw the output? This page builds the mental model from first principles—with a paintable canvas, a live flood-fill, and real per-pixel metrics.

~15 min Computer Vision · Segmentation
Prelude

Three flavors of "segmentation"

The word means different things depending on who says it:

  • Semantic segmentation—every pixel gets a class label (sky, road, person). Two adjacent people share a label: we don't care that they are separate.
  • Instance segmentation—every pixel gets a label and an instance ID. We do care that there are three separate people: they get three separate masks.
  • Panoptic segmentation—the union of the two. "Things" (countable: person, car) get instance IDs, "stuff" (uncountable: sky, road) just gets a class label. Every pixel is labeled exactly once.

Step 2

A segmentation is a function from pixels to labels

For an image of width $W$ and height $H$ with $C$ classes, the ground truth is a $W \times H$ array of integers from 0 to $C-1$. A model's output is a $W \times H \times C$ tensor of class probabilities; taking the argmax per pixel collapses it back to a label map.

Step 3

Region growing: a pre-neural primitive

Before deep learning, most segmentation started from a seed pixel and "grew" outward until pixels stopped being similar enough. The rule is simple: include a neighbor if its color is close to the seed's (or the running average of the region). Ignore it otherwise.

Where it breaks. Drop a seed on the sky and raise $\tau$ slowly. At low $\tau$ you get a clean sky mask; at high $\tau$, the region leaks into adjacent buildings. That's the core weakness of classical segmentation: it has no semantics. "Close in colour" is a fragile proxy for "same object".
Step 4

How neural networks cheat better

A modern segmenter is essentially a function that maps $H \times W \times 3$ pixels to $H \times W \times C$ class logits. Three architectural ideas dominate:

Family Key move Representatives Strength
Encoder-decoder CNN downsamples to a small feature map, then upsamples back to full resolution. FCN, U-Net, SegNet Combines coarse semantics and fine detail via skip connections.
Dilated / multi-scale Keep resolution high; expand receptive field with dilated convolutions and pyramid pooling. DeepLab v1–v3+, PSPNet Sees both the tree and the forest without losing pixel precision.
Mask-prediction / Transformer Predict a set of (class, binary mask) pairs via decoder queries; no per-pixel argmax. Mask R-CNN, MaskFormer, SAM Natively does instance & panoptic — each mask is one object.
Why "combine semantic info and fine detail" is hard. To recognise that a pixel belongs to a car, a network needs a big receptive field (looks at many neighbouring pixels, many layers deep). To place the boundary of the car on the right pixel, it needs high spatial resolution. These two goals fight each other; every architecture above is one particular truce.
Step 5

Pick the right loss

Per-pixel cross-entropy is the obvious starting point, but it fails spectacularly on imbalanced classes (most images are 80% background). Three losses fix that:

  • Dice loss (Milletari et al., 2016): maximize the soft Dice coefficient between predicted and target masks. It penalizes class imbalance naturally because both numerator and denominator shrink together.
  • IoU loss: directly optimize the very metric that benchmarks score you on. Numerically trickier than Dice but mathematically closer to what you want.
  • Focal loss (Lin et al., 2017): cross-entropy reweighted to focus on hard pixels; mostly a detection trick but often used for heavily imbalanced segmentation too.
Step 6

The big number

An image of 640×400 pixels is 256,000 individual per-pixel predictions. A ViT patch classifier has a few hundred outputs per image. A YOLO detector has maybe a few thousand anchor decisions. Segmentation is roughly three orders of magnitude denser.

Every single one has to get a class. A model that's 99% accurate still gets 2,560 pixels wrong — which often shows up as jagged edges and missed thin structures.

Step 7

Four things to get wrong

Myth

"Pixel accuracy tells you if the model is good."
On a scene that's 95% sky, labeling everything as "sky" gives 95% pixel accuracy and zero useful output. Always report per-class or mean IoU next to pixel accuracy.

Myth

"Bigger masks are always better."
Just like in detection, a mask that's a strict superset of the truth has huge union and loses IoU fast. Tight is right; over-shooting the boundary is penalised as much as missing it.

Myth

"Segmentation is just classification, done more."
It is in the "per-pixel argmax" framing. But a pure per-pixel classifier produces flickery, isolated mislabeled pixels. All modern architectures inject structural priors (skip connections, CRFs, attention) to enforce coherent regions.

Myth

"IoU 0.9 looks perfect."
It can still miss a 10px-wide telephone wire threading across the whole image—and that wire might be the only thing your autopilot needs to notice. Always look at qualitative failures beside the mean-IoU headline.

Final takeaway. Segmentation pushes the same ML primitives (classification, thresholding, losses) down to every pixel. You've now painted a mask yourself, measured it against ground truth, and watched a pre-neural region-grower succeed and fail—exactly the two modes modern CNNs and Transformers are designed to fix. The "magic" of Mask R-CNN and SAM is just these ideas, with bigger receptive fields and better features.
