From a line to an ellipse

A multivariate normal is just a familiar bell curve seen from more than one direction.

In one dimension, a normal distribution tells you where points tend to land on a line. In two dimensions, it tells you where points tend to land on a plane. The shape becomes a cloud, but the intuition stays the same: a center, a spread, and a preference for some directions over others.

The reason people keep coming back to this family is not just that it is mathematically neat. It is that Gaussian structure makes uncertainty feel geometric. You can see the mean. You can see the spread. You can see what happens when one variable starts whispering information about another.

Section 1

One bell, three knobs

The one-dimensional normal is the base case. The center is μ, the width is σ, and the shaded interval shows how much probability mass sits between two points.

Distribution

X ~ N(μ, σ²)

Read this as: the random variable X is Gaussian with mean μ and variance σ².

Moments

E[X] = μ
Var(X) = σ²

The mean sets the center of mass. The variance sets the squared width of the uncertainty.

Density

f(x) = 1 / (σ√(2π)) exp(-(x - μ)² / (2σ²))

The formula matters less than the pattern: farther from μ means exponentially less density.

Pull μ left or right and the whole curve slides with it. Increase σ and the curve spreads out: the peak drops because the same total probability must be spread across a wider range.

The yellow region is not “height”; it is area under the curve. That area is the probability that a draw lands inside the chosen interval.

f(x) = 1 / (σ√(2π)) exp(-(x - μ)² / (2σ²))

P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = Φ((b - μ) / σ) - Φ((a - μ) / σ)

A good real-world picture is exam scores around a class average, or measurement error around the true value of a sensor. The mean tells you the center of the story; the standard deviation tells you how noisy that story is.

Exam scores

If μ is the class average, then σ is how spread out the class is. A small σ means most students cluster near the same score.

Sensor noise

If a sensor measures the same temperature repeatedly, the bell curve describes the measurement error around the true reading.

The 68-95-99 rule

When you center the interval around the mean, one, two, and three standard deviations collect about 68%, 95%, and 99.7% of the mass.

Section 2

From a curve to a cloud

In two dimensions, the normal distribution becomes a hill over the plane. Seen from above, equal-density contours look like ellipses. Their tilt and stretch come from the covariance matrix.

Vector form

[X, Y]T ~ N(μ, Σ)

The mean becomes a vector and the spread becomes a matrix. That is the whole conceptual jump from 1D to 2D.

Covariance matrix

Σ = [[σx², ρσxσy], [ρσxσy, σy²]]

The diagonal entries are the axis variances. The off-diagonal entry is the covariance Cov(X, Y) = ρσxσy.

2D density

f(z) = 1 / (2π|Σ|1/2) exp(-½(z - μ)TΣ-1(z - μ))

The determinant |Σ| controls how spread out the mass is, while Σ-1 defines the ellipse geometry.

If ρ = 0, the cloud is axis-aligned: knowing x tells you nothing about y. As |ρ| grows, the cloud leans. Positive correlation tilts upward. Negative correlation tilts downward.

The sample dots are draws from the current Gaussian. The nested ellipses are contour lines: each one collects points with the same Mahalanobis distance from the mean.

A practical way to read the covariance matrix is this: the diagonal terms σx² and σy² tell you how wide the cloud wants to be along each axis, while the shared term Cov(X, Y) = ρσxσy tells you whether large values of one variable tend to arrive with large values of the other.

Σ = [[σx², ρσxσy], [ρσxσy, σy²]]

(z - μ)TΣ-1(z - μ) = c

The first line tells you how the cloud is built. The second tells you why contours become ellipses: points with equal quadratic distance from the center share the same density.

Height and weight

Positive correlation means taller people tend to weigh more. The cloud leans upward because the two variables tend to move together.

Speed and travel time

For a fixed trip length, higher speed means lower time. That creates a downward tilt: one variable rises as the other falls.

Tracking uncertainty

When a robot is unsure about where an object lies along a corridor, the Gaussian becomes a long thin ellipse instead of a round blob.

Section 3

A few concrete pictures to carry around

Abstract parameters stick better when they point to something concrete. These presets package the same Gaussian ideas into recognizable situations.

Student performance

Imagine x as hours of sleep and y as quiz score. More sleep tends to help, but not perfectly, so you get a positively tilted cloud rather than a line.

Budget and surprise costs

Let x be planned spending and y be leftover cash. Bigger plans often leave less cash, so the cloud tilts downward.

GPS uncertainty on a road

The location is much more uncertain along the road than across it. That creates a thin ridge: one very uncertain direction, one very certain direction.

The hinge

Once the cloud feels concrete, two questions matter more than the raw density.

If you ignore one axis, what uncertainty remains? If you pin one axis to a value, how does the other one respond? For Gaussians, both answers stay inside the same family, which is why these distributions show up everywhere from Kalman filters to regression.

Marginal = forget one axis Conditional = fix one axis Both stay Gaussian

Section 4

Marginals forget. Conditionals slice.

A marginal ignores one axis entirely. A conditional fixes one axis and asks what remains on the other. For a Gaussian, both operations stay Gaussian, which is why this family is so useful.

Joint Model

[X, Y]T ~ N([0.00, 0.00]T, [[1.44, 0.59], [0.59, 0.81]])

Everything below comes from this one Gaussian. Marginals integrate out an axis. Conditionals pin one axis to a value and read off the remaining uncertainty.

Step 1

See the marginals first

The top curve is what remains if you forget y. The right curve is what remains if you forget x. Nothing is sliced yet.

X marginal

X ~ N(0.00, 1.20²)

Marginal Math

fX(x) = ∫ fX,Y(x, y) dy

fY(y) = ∫ fX,Y(x, y) dx

A marginal keeps the axis you care about and blends over the other one. For a Gaussian, the result is still Gaussian.

X Axis

X ~ N(0.00, 1.20²)

Only μx and σx² matter here. Correlation does not change the marginal width of x.

Y Axis

Y ~ N(0.00, 0.90²)

The y marginal depends on μy and σy², not on the tilt of the cloud.

What a Marginal Ignores

Changing ρ rotates the joint cloud, but the teal curves stay anchored to the diagonal of Σ.

That is why the marginal plots are the cleanest place to see what the variances alone are doing.

Step 2

Now drag a slice line and watch the conditional appear

Move the vertical line left or right to control Y | X = x0. Move the horizontal line up or down to control X | Y = y0. Drag the white intersection point to move both together.

The teal curves are the marginals. Gold shows X | Y = y0. Coral shows Y | X = x0.

Along the x-axis

Teal: X marginal. Gold: X | Y = 0.00.

Conditional Math

X | Y = y0 ~ N(μx + ρ(σx/σy)(y0 - μy), (1 - ρ²)σx²)

Y | X = x0 ~ N(μy + ρ(σy/σx)(x0 - μx), (1 - ρ²)σy²)

Conditionals keep the Gaussian form. The center slides linearly; the variance shrinks by the factor 1 - ρ².

Observed slices

x0 = 0.00, y0 = 0.00

These are the highlighted vertical and horizontal cuts through the joint density.

Top Axis: X and X | Y

X ~ N(0.00, 1.20²)
X | Y = 0.00 ~ N(0.00, 1.00²)

The gold curve lives on the same x-axis as the teal marginal, so the shrinkage is visually honest.

Right Axis: Y and Y | X

Y ~ N(0.00, 0.90²)
Y | X = 0.00 ~ N(0.00, 0.75²)

The coral curve uses the same y coordinates as the main plot, so the conditional mean lands exactly where the slice predicts.

What Changes, What Doesn't

Std shrink factor = 0.84

Marginals keep their original widths. Conditionals get narrower as correlation gets stronger.

Section 5

A few good things to notice

These quick patterns are where the intuition tends to click. Tap one, then adjust the sliders yourself.

Correlation rotates, not just squeezes

Keep both spreads fixed and slide ρ. Watch the same cloud pivot from downward to upward tilt.

Marginals ignore tilt

Change ρ while looking at the x and y marginals. Their widths do not change, because marginals only care about the diagonal of the covariance matrix.

Conditionals get tighter with stronger correlation

Push |ρ| toward 1 and the conditional distribution becomes sharply concentrated around a line.
