From a line to an ellipse

A multivariate normal is just a familiar bell curve seen from more than one direction.

Think of exam scores clustering around a class average, GPS estimates clustering around a true location, or day-to-day measurement error clustering around the truth. In one dimension, a normal distribution tells you where points tend to land on a line. In two dimensions, it tells you where points tend to land on a plane. The shape becomes a cloud, but the intuition stays the same: a center, a spread, and a preference for some directions over others.

The reason people keep coming back to this family is not just that it is mathematically neat. It is that Gaussian structure makes uncertainty feel geometric. You can see the mean. You can see the spread. You can see what happens when one variable starts whispering information about another. That makes the Gaussian a useful language for data, noise, prediction, and estimation.

Keep these three questions in view

  • Where is the center?
  • How wide is the uncertainty?
  • If one coordinate moves, what does the other one tend to do?

The article follows one path all the way through. First: what does a bell curve mean in one variable? Next: how does that become a tilted cloud in two variables? Then: how do you draw real samples from that cloud, and how do you fit the cloud back from observed points? Only after that do marginals and conditionals start to feel natural.

Before the formulas

Why this family keeps showing up

The Gaussian is not important because reality is perfectly bell-shaped. It is important because many ordinary uncertainty stories collapse into the same geometry: a center, a spread, and sometimes a shared direction.

Start with the simplest measurement story. There is a true quantity you care about, and then there is noise: little nudges from imperfect sensors, timing jitter, rounding, motion, or human variation. If those nudges come from many small effects rather than one giant shock, the final error often looks close to Gaussian.

The same pattern survives in higher dimensions. A location estimate is really an uncertainty cloud around a true position. A pair of related measurements is really a cloud around a typical pair of values. If both coordinates are being pushed around by the same hidden causes, the cloud tilts.

\[ \text{measurement} = \text{truth} + \text{noise} \]
\[ Z = \mu + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma) \]

Those two lines are the whole article in compressed form. The mean μ is the typical location. The covariance Σ says how uncertainty stretches, tilts, and couples coordinates together.

Repeated measurements

Measure the same temperature again and again. The readings bounce around the true value. In one dimension, that bounce is a bell curve.

Location estimates

A phone GPS does not output the exact location. It outputs a cloud of likely positions around the truth. In two dimensions, that cloud is the natural object.

Related quantities

Hours of sleep and quiz score, speed and travel time, or two drifting machine measurements all produce clouds with a preferred tilt. That tilt is covariance made visible.

Prediction from partial information

Once you observe one coordinate, the other is less mysterious. That is why conditional Gaussians feel like the visual ancestor of regression.

The plan from here is simple. First get comfortable with a bell curve on a line. Then upgrade the line to a plane. Then watch three operations that practitioners use constantly: sampling, fitting, and slicing.

Section 1

One bell, three knobs

The one-dimensional normal is the base case. The center is μ, the width is σ, and the shaded interval shows how much probability mass sits between two points.

Distribution

\[ X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \]

Read this as: the random variable X is Gaussian with mean μ and variance σ².

Moments

\[ \mathbb{E}[X] = \mu, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(X) = \sigma^2 \]

The mean sets the center of mass. The variance sets the squared width of the uncertainty.

Density

\[ f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left( -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \]

The formula matters less than the pattern: farther from μ means exponentially less density.

Pull μ left or right and the whole curve slides with it. Increase σ and the curve spreads out: the peak drops because the same total probability must be spread across a wider range.

The yellow region is not “height”; it is area under the curve. That area is the probability that a draw lands inside the chosen interval.

This is the first place where people often get tripped up. The curve is not a histogram of observed counts. It is an idealized density. The only thing with probabilistic meaning is area, not the height at one exact point.

\[ f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \]
\[ \mathbb{P}(a \le X \le b) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right) - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma}\right) \]

A good real-world picture is exam scores around a class average, or measurement error around the true value of a sensor. The mean tells you the center of the story; the standard deviation tells you how noisy that story is.

If you keep this interpretation straight, the jump to higher dimensions becomes much easier. You are not learning a new species of object in 2D. You are still describing where observations tend to gather, only now there are multiple directions in which they can spread.

The standardized coordinate z = (x-μ)/σ is worth keeping in mind because it removes the units and tells you how surprising a point is relative to the bell. Most of the later formulas in the article are just higher-dimensional versions of the same “recenter, then rescale” idea.

The bell gets wider as σ grows, and the peak drops because total probability must still add up to 1.

Section 2

From a curve to a cloud

In two dimensions, the normal distribution becomes a hill over the plane. Seen from above, equal-density contours look like ellipses. Their tilt and stretch come from the covariance matrix.

Vector form

\[ \begin{bmatrix} X \\ Y \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mu_x \\ \mu_y \end{bmatrix}, \Sigma \right) \]

The mean becomes a vector and the spread becomes a matrix. That is the whole conceptual jump from 1D to 2D.

Covariance matrix

\[ \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x^2 & \rho \sigma_x \sigma_y \\ \rho \sigma_x \sigma_y & \sigma_y^2 \end{bmatrix} \]

The diagonal entries are the axis variances. The off-diagonal entry is the covariance Cov(X, Y) = ρσxσy.

2D density

\[ \begin{aligned} f_Z(z) &= \frac{1}{2\pi |\Sigma|^{1/2}} \\ &\quad \times \exp\!\left( -\frac{1}{2}(z-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(z-\mu) \right) \end{aligned} \]

The determinant |Σ| controls how spread out the mass is, while Σ-1 defines the ellipse geometry.

Ellipse view

\[ \Sigma = Q \Lambda Q^\top, \qquad \Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Rotate into the eigenvector basis and the cloud becomes axis-aligned. The eigenvalues are the squared spreads along the cloud's natural directions.

If ρ = 0, the cloud is axis-aligned: knowing x tells you nothing about y. As |ρ| grows, the cloud leans. Positive correlation tilts upward. Negative correlation tilts downward.

The sample dots are draws from the current Gaussian. The nested ellipses are contour lines: each one collects points with the same Mahalanobis distance from the mean.

A practical way to read the covariance matrix is this: the diagonal terms σx² and σy² tell you how wide the cloud wants to be along each axis, while the shared term Cov(X, Y) = ρσxσy tells you whether large values of one variable tend to arrive with large values of the other.

Another way to say the same thing is geometric: the covariance matrix chooses the shape of the ellipse that best summarizes uncertainty. Round means all directions are equally uncertain. Long and thin means one direction is much less certain than another. Tilt means the variables do not vary independently.

That is the practical reading rule for covariance matrices: they do not just summarize pairwise dependence. They define a coordinate system in which the Gaussian becomes easy to reason about. Rotate to the right basis and the cloud stops looking mysterious.

\[ \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x^2 & \rho \sigma_x \sigma_y \\ \rho \sigma_x \sigma_y & \sigma_y^2 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ (z-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(z-\mu) = c \]

The first line tells you how the cloud is built. The second tells you why contours become ellipses: points with equal quadratic distance from the center share the same density.

If you are coming from linear algebra, this is the moment to notice that covariance is not just a table of numbers. It defines a quadratic form. The ellipse is the visual trace of that quadratic form in the plane.

The dots are random draws. The ellipses are equal-density contours. Correlation changes their tilt; variances change their width.

Section 3

Sampling: how the cloud becomes points

A Gaussian is not just a contour plot. It is a recipe for generating actual points. Start with a standard normal, stretch and rotate it, then shift it by the mean.

In practice, sampling from a multivariate normal means drawing a simple isotropic vector first, then transforming it so it picks up the mean and covariance you want.

This matters because it turns a fancy-looking model into a very concrete procedure. Start with circular uncertainty. Then stretch it, rotate it, and shift it. The multivariate normal is not mysterious once you see it as a transformed standard normal.

\[ z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \]
\[ x = \mu + Lz, \qquad LL^\top = \Sigma \]

The outline below is the current Gaussian from the 2D controls above. The points are actual draws from that model. Increase the sample size and the cloud starts to look more like the target ellipse.

Small samples wobble. They can look lopsided, off-center, or more correlated than the true model. That is not a bug. It is what sampling noise looks like. The whole reason we estimate parameters from data is that finite samples never match the ideal cloud exactly.

Treat the sample mean and sample covariance as random objects too. If you redraw the sample, those estimates move. With enough data they calm down, but at small n they wander in exactly the way the figure shows.

Section 4

Learning the parameters from a toy dataset

Once you have data, the Gaussian can be fit with two summaries: the average location and the average spread around that location.

For a Gaussian, the core estimates are simple. The mean is the average of the points. The covariance is the average outer product of the centered points. These are the maximum-likelihood estimates for the model.

Intuitively, the mean asks: where is the center of the cloud? The covariance asks: once I recenter the cloud, how does it spread out, and in which directions does it spread together? Those two summaries are enough to reconstruct the full multivariate normal.

\[ \hat{\mu} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i \]
\[ \hat{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\hat{\mu})(x_i-\hat{\mu})^\top \]

Pick a small toy dataset. The plot shows the observed points, a fitted ellipse from the estimated parameters, and a faint dashed ellipse for the hidden Gaussian that generated the data.

This is the simplest form of statistical learning in the article. We do not know the true parameters, so we infer them from points. If the fit looks wrong, it is because the data are small, noisy, or both.

The table next to the figure is the actual dataset being fit. Nothing is hidden behind a magical optimizer here. The estimate is just “average the points, then average the centered outer products.” That simplicity is one reason Gaussians are such durable teaching examples.

Section 5

Six stories told with the same Gaussian language

The distribution does not care whether the axes mean sleep, money, location, or measurement drift. What changes is the story you map onto the same geometry.

Load any of these into the 2D plot above. The point of the examples is not that the world is exactly Gaussian. The point is that the Gaussian is a compact language for center, spread, and co-movement.

This is why the same math reappears in so many places. Once you know how to read one Gaussian cloud, you can mentally translate between very different application stories.

In practice, the Gaussian is often a first approximation rather than a final truth. But it is a very useful first approximation because it gives you something geometric to inspect and something algebraic to compute with.

Positive tilt

Sleep and quiz score

x is hours of sleep. y is quiz score. More sleep usually helps, but not perfectly, so the cloud leans upward instead of collapsing to a line.

Takeaway: correlation is a tendency, not a rigid law.

Negative tilt

Budget and leftover cash

Takeaway: a negative covariance means one variable tends to rise while the other falls.

Long thin ridge

GPS uncertainty on a road

The position is much more uncertain along the road than across it. That produces a thin ridge: one direction is loose, the other direction is tight.

Takeaway: the ellipse shows uncertainty in the coordinates the system actually cares about, not just in the raw x and y axes.

Shared trend

Height and weight

x is height. y is weight. Taller people tend to weigh more, so the cloud leans upward and the best slice-based prediction also moves upward.

Takeaway: conditioning is the move from “people in general” to “people near this x-value.”

Tradeoff

Speed and travel time

x is average speed. y is travel time for a fixed route. Faster trips tend to take less time, so the cloud leans downward.

Takeaway: negative correlation is easiest to feel when the story naturally contains a tradeoff.

Common drift

Two measurements from one drifting machine

Imagine width and thickness from the same production line. If the machine drifts, both measurements drift together and the cloud becomes narrow along one preferred direction.

Takeaway: covariance often encodes a hidden shared cause.

The hinge

Once the cloud feels concrete, two questions matter more than the raw density.

If you ignore one axis, what uncertainty remains? If you pin one axis to a value, how does the other one respond? For Gaussians, both answers stay inside the same family, which is why these distributions show up everywhere from Kalman filters to regression.

\[ f_X(x) = \int f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy \]
\[ f_{X \mid Y=y_0}(x) \propto f_{X,Y}(x,y_0) \]

Section 6

Marginals forget. Conditionals slice.

A marginal ignores one axis entirely. A conditional fixes one axis and asks what remains on the other. For a Gaussian, both operations stay Gaussian, which is why this family is so useful.

This is the payoff section. Up to now, the Gaussian has been a static object: a curve, then a cloud, then a sampling recipe, then a fitted model. Marginals and conditionals are where that object starts answering questions. What remains if I ignore one coordinate? What changes if I observe one coordinate?

The main reason Gaussians dominate so many workflows is closure: you start Gaussian, you marginalize, you condition, and you are still Gaussian. The algebra stays manageable and the picture stays readable.

Joint Model

Everything below comes from this one Gaussian. Marginals integrate out an axis. Conditionals pin one axis to a value and read off the remaining uncertainty.

\[ f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy \]
\[ f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx \]

A marginal keeps the axis you care about and blends over the other one. For a Gaussian, the result is still Gaussian.

Conditional Math

\[ X \mid Y=y_0 : \quad \begin{aligned} \mu_{X \mid Y=y_0} &= \mu_x + \rho\frac{\sigma_x}{\sigma_y}(y_0-\mu_y) \\ \sigma^2_{X \mid Y=y_0} &= (1-\rho^2)\sigma_x^2 \end{aligned} \]
\[ Y \mid X=x_0 : \quad \begin{aligned} \mu_{Y \mid X=x_0} &= \mu_y + \rho\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x}(x_0-\mu_x) \\ \sigma^2_{Y \mid X=x_0} &= (1-\rho^2)\sigma_y^2 \end{aligned} \]

Conditionals keep the Gaussian form. The center slides linearly; the variance shrinks by the factor 1 - ρ².

Section 7

What to keep in your head

If you forget the exact formulas, these are the pictures and rules worth keeping.

1D first

In one dimension, the Gaussian is just a center and a width. Probability lives in area, not in the height at a single point.

Then geometry

In two dimensions, the mean becomes a point and the covariance matrix becomes an ellipse. Diagonal entries set axis spread; off-diagonal entries create tilt.

Sampling is concrete

To draw from a multivariate normal, start with a simple standard normal vector and transform it. The cloud is a recipe, not just a picture.

Learning is simple

Fit the Gaussian by averaging the points and then averaging centered outer products. The difficulty is usually small data, not complicated formulas.

Marginals forget

When you integrate out one axis, you keep only the mean and variance of the axis you care about. Correlation changes the joint cloud, not the marginal widths.

Conditionals focus

When you observe one coordinate, the other coordinate stays Gaussian but becomes narrower. Stronger correlation means sharper conditional uncertainty.

If you remember one picture, remember this: a multivariate normal is a smooth cloud with a center, a preferred spread, and a tilt. Marginals look at the cloud from the side. Conditionals cut through it and become narrower when the variables agree.

If you remember one workflow, remember this: write down a mean and covariance, sample points, estimate the mean and covariance back from those points, then ask marginal and conditional questions. That loop is the full story in miniature.
