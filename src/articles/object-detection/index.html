Object Detection, from Boxes Up

Classification asks "what is in this image?" Object detection asks "what and where?"—and answers with bounding boxes. This page strips detection down to its plumbing: boxes, Intersection over Union, confidence thresholds, precision/recall trade-offs, and non-max suppression—each one computed live on a scene you pick.

~14 min Computer Vision · Object Detection
Prelude

What does a detector actually output?

A modern object detector ingests an image and emits a list of rows. Each row looks like this:

Four numbers for the bounding box, a class label (cat, car, person…), and a confidence score in $[0, 1]$. That's it. Everything clever in object detection—anchors, feature pyramids, DETR's set loss—is just about producing that list well. Before worrying about how the list is produced, you need a crisp understanding of how it's scored. That's the job of this page.

Pick a scene

Step 1

A bounding box is four numbers

A bounding box is just $(x_1, y_1, x_2, y_2)$: the top-left and bottom-right corners. People also write it as $(x, y, w, h)$ (center + size) or $(c_x, c_y, w, h)$. All three are equivalent and trivially convertible. Area is width × height. Two boxes overlap if and only if both their x-ranges and y-ranges overlap.

Step 2

Intersection over Union (IoU)

The single most important measurement in detection. IoU asks: what fraction of the combined area do the two boxes agree on?

IoU is always between 0 (no overlap) and 1 (exact match). Two boxes that cover nearly the same region score near 1. Two boxes that barely touch score near 0. A common convention: a prediction is a "true positive" if its IoU with some ground-truth box exceeds a threshold (typically 0.5).

The 0.5 threshold is a convention, not a law. COCO averages AP over IoU thresholds from 0.5 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05 precisely because a single threshold rewards lazy, slightly-off boxes as much as pixel-perfect ones.

Step 4

Non-Max Suppression (NMS)

Even with a sane confidence threshold, you end up with clusters of near-duplicate boxes all firing on the same object. Non-Max Suppression is the classic deduplication rule:

  1. Sort all surviving detections by confidence, highest first.
  2. Take the top box and "keep" it.
  3. Discard every other box whose IoU with the kept box is above the NMS threshold.
  4. Go back to step 2 with the remaining candidates.

NMS gets it wrong on crowded scenes. Switch to the Crowded pedestrians scene. Two people close together have boxes with genuine high IoU—but those are different people, not duplicates. Classic NMS will kill one of them. This is why modern detectors use Soft-NMS, matching costs (DETR), or NMS-free designs.
Step 5

Where do the boxes actually come from?

We've been evaluating detections. Let's zoom out on how a model proposes them. Three families dominate:

Family How it proposes boxes Representatives Gotcha
Anchor-based Place a dense grid of "anchor" boxes of many sizes/aspect ratios across the image; for each, predict a class and a $(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta w, \Delta h)$ offset. Faster R-CNN, SSD, RetinaNet, YOLOv3/v4/v5 Anchor hyperparameters matter a lot. Needs NMS.
Anchor-free / point-based For each pixel (or each cell of a reduced feature map), predict "am I the centre of an object?" plus a box relative to that point. FCOS, CenterNet, YOLOX, YOLOv8 Needs clever loss weighting to avoid trivial "no object" predictions everywhere.
Set-based / DETR Fixed-size set of learned object queries (usually 100). Each query attends to image features and outputs (class, box). Matched to ground truth by Hungarian algorithm. DETR, Deformable DETR, DINO No NMS needed. Trains slowly; small-object performance originally weak.

Step 6

The final reveal: mean Average Precision

Benchmarks like COCO don't care about a single threshold. They sweep the confidence threshold from 1 down to 0, plot precision against recall, and integrate the area under that curve. That's Average Precision (AP). Average across classes → mean AP (mAP). COCO additionally averages AP across IoU thresholds from 0.5 to 0.95.

Step 7

Four gotchas beginners run into

Myth

"A 99% confidence prediction must be right."
Modern detectors are miscalibrated. High confidence correlates with rightness, but two detectors with the same average accuracy can have wildly different calibration. Always evaluate with a real metric, not by eyeballing confidences.

Myth

"mAP is all I need to compare detectors."
mAP averages away the thing you might care about most: maybe you want 100% recall even at ugly precision (medical screening), or 100% precision at any recall (autopilot). Plot the full PR curve before picking a model.

Myth

"NMS is just cleanup; it doesn't matter much."
NMS thresholds can swing mAP by 2–3 points on hard benchmarks. Worse: NMS deletes real duplicate objects (crowds!). For crowded data, Soft-NMS or NMS-free designs routinely beat classic NMS.

Myth

"Bigger boxes always win at IoU."
A prediction that is a strict superset of the truth has IoU = truth / pred, which drops as pred grows. One common failure: predictor emits a huge box "to be safe"—IoU plummets. Tighter is better, as long as you still cover the object.

Final takeaway. Object detection is classification + localization + list deduplication. IoU is the scissors that cuts overlapping boxes down to one; precision and recall tell you whether you cut too little or too much; mAP compresses everything into one number to watch rise during training. Every modern detector—YOLO, DETR, Faster R-CNN—is one of these primitives plus a bigger backbone and a smarter decoder.
