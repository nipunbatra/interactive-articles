The Optimizer Race

Deep learning models are trained by rolling down a hill (a "loss landscape"). Let's see why standard gradient descent gets stuck, and how Momentum and Adam were designed to fix it.

~15 min Deep Learning · Optimization · SGD
Part I

The Landscape

Imagine training a neural network with two parameters ($x$ and $y$). The height of the terrain is the loss (error). Our goal is to reach the lowest point (the minimum).

Below, we will explore a classic "ravine" optimization problem (similar to Beale's function or Rosenbrock's valley). The ravine slopes gently towards the minimum, but its sides are incredibly steep. We can define this mathematically as:

Moving along the $y$-axis is very steep, but moving along the $x$-axis is very flat. This unevenness is what makes neural networks so notoriously difficult to train.

Part II

Why SGD struggles

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is the simplest optimization algorithm. At every step, it calculates the slope (the gradient) and takes a step directly downhill.

  • $W_t$: Our current position (weights).
  • $\alpha$: The Learning Rate (step size).
  • $\nabla L(W_t)$: The gradient (slope) at our current position.

In a ravine, "downhill" mostly points straight across the steep walls, not along the flat floor. So SGD spends all its energy bouncing violently wall-to-wall, making agonizingly slow progress toward the minimum.

Part III

Momentum: Adding Mass

What if our optimizer had physical mass? Momentum remembers the direction of previous steps. It acts like a heavy ball rolling down the hill.

  • $V_t$: Our current velocity.
  • $\beta$: A friction factor (usually 0.9) that slowly decays old velocity.

Because the gradients point back and forth across the ravine walls, those perpendicular forces cancel each other out in the velocity vector. Meanwhile, the tiny consistent push down the flat floor accumulates, building up speed exactly where we want it!

Part IV

Adam: Smart Scaling

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) goes a step further. What if we could magically warp the landscape so the steep walls flatter and the flat floor steeper?

  • $m_{t+1}$: The momentum (average of past gradients).
  • $v_{t+1}$: The variance (average of past squared gradients).

Adam divides the step size by the square root of the variance ($\sqrt{v_{t+1}}$). This is genius: gradients along the steep walls have high variance, so Adam dynamically shrinks the learning rate there. Gradients along the floor have low variance, so Adam dynamically boosts the learning rate there. It creates a smooth, direct path to the minimum.
