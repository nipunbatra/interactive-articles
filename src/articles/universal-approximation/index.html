A Visual Proof That One Layer Can Compute Anything

The Universal Approximation Theorem says a neural net with a single hidden layer can mimic almost any function. This page rebuilds Michael Nielsen's beautiful visual argument—you'll flatten a sigmoid into a step, glue two steps into a bump, design a whole function by hand, and then stack towers to fit a surface. No proof, just pictures you can grab.

~18 min Deep Learning · Neural Networks · Approximation
Prelude

The claim that sounds too good

Here is one of the most quoted results in machine learning: a feed-forward network with a single hidden layer can approximate any continuous function to any accuracy, given enough neurons. One layer. Any function. People repeat it like a slogan—but almost nobody can see why it's true.

We're going to make it visible, following Michael Nielsen's construction from Chapter 4 of Neural Networks and Deep Learning. Two honest caveats first, because the theorem is narrower than the slogan:

Caveat 1 — approximate, not exact. We won't compute a function perfectly. We'll get arbitrarily close—and we make the gap smaller by adding more neurons.
Caveat 2 — continuous functions only. The target has to be continuous (no instantaneous jumps). That's the class one hidden layer can handle.

With those in hand, the whole trick is to stop thinking of the hidden layer as “a network” and start thinking of it as a kit of interchangeable bricks. Let's forge the first brick.

Step 2

Crank the weight → a step function

When the weight is enormous, the sigmoid stops looking like a gentle S and snaps into a step function: $0$ on the left, $1$ on the right, with a near-vertical cliff. That's a huge simplification—a step is described by a single number: the position of its cliff.

The cliff sits where the sigmoid's input is zero, i.e. $wx + b = 0$, which means $x = -b/w$. Nielsen's key move is to rename the neuron by this position. Define the step position

The payoff. From here on, every hidden neuron is just “a step at position $s$.” One number per neuron. That's the vocabulary we'll build everything else from.
Step 3

Two steps make a bump

One step is monotone—it goes up and stays up. To get something local, take two steps and subtract. Put a step at $s_1$ with output weight $+h$, and a step at $s_2 > s_1$ with output weight $-h$. Add them:

To the left of $s_1$ both steps are $0$, so the sum is $0$. Between $s_1$ and $s_2$ the first step is “on” but the second isn't, so the sum is $h$. After $s_2$ both are on and they cancel back to $0$. The result is a bump of height $h$ sitting on the interval $[s_1, s_2]$. This is our brick.

Play. Move the edges together for a narrow spike, apart for a wide plateau. Push $h$ negative to flip the bump downward. With this one brick—an adjustable rectangle—you can already feel where this is going.

The subtlety hiding in the output (press “Show the σ⁻¹ goal”). A real neuron's output layer squashes its weighted input through one more sigmoid, so the network actually computes $\sigma\!\big(H(x)\big)$, not $H(x)$. To make $\sigma(H(x)) \approx f(x)$ we need $H(x) \approx \sigma^{-1}\!\big(f(x)\big)$, the inverse sigmoid (logit) of the goal. So the heights aren't tracing $f$ directly—they're tracing $\sigma^{-1}\!\circ f$. Toggle the view to design against the honest target; the bumps still line up one-per-slab.
More bumps, smaller gap. Crank $N$ up and re-fit. The staircase gets finer and the deviation shrinks toward zero. That's the entire theorem in one slider: accuracy is bought with width.
Step 5

Two inputs: build a tower

Real functions usually have more than one input. With inputs $x$ and $y$, a high-weight neuron that only listens to $x$ makes a step wall running along $y$; one that only listens to $y$ makes a wall running along $x$. Pair them up as before and you get a bump in $x$ (a ridge) and a bump in $y$ (a perpendicular ridge).

Add the two ridges. Where neither is on, the sum is $0$; on a single ridge it's $h$; where they cross, it's $2h$. That cross is the left panel below. Now apply one more sigmoid with a threshold set between $h$ and $2h$: everything below the threshold gets crushed to $0$, only the central $2h$ patch survives. The ridges vanish and you're left with a single tower — a localized 2-D bump (right panel).

Why $1.5h$? It's the only level that separates “both ridges on” ($2h$) from “just one” ($h$). Set the threshold too low and the ridge arms leak through; too high and even the center dies. The tower is what's left when you keep only the overlap.
Step 6

Tile towers into any surface

A tower is a movable, resizable block of adjustable height. Cover the plane with a grid of them and set each tower's height to the value of the target surface at that spot. The sum is a 3-D “staircase” that hugs the surface — and just like in 1-D, a finer grid means a closer fit. That's universal approximation for functions of two variables, and the same move tiles into any number of inputs.

Count the cost. Each tower needs $4$ hidden neurons (two ridges × two steps). A $6\times6$ grid is $36$ towers — $144$ neurons — for a coarse fit. Push the resolution up and the neuron count explodes. The theorem guarantees a fit exists; it says nothing about it being cheap.
Step 7

What the proof does not say

You've now built the whole argument: step → bump → designed function → tower → tiled surface. It's genuinely a proof of universality. But four things get lost when it's flattened into a slogan:

Myth

“One hidden layer is enough in practice.”
Enough width suffices in principle — but the width can be astronomical. Our tiling used $4$ neurons per tower and still only gave a blocky fit. Depth lets a network build bumps from bumps and reuse parts, which is why real networks are deep, not absurdly wide.

Myth

“Training will find this network.”
We placed every step and height by hand. The proof shows a good network exists; it's silent on whether gradient descent can discover it. Existence and learnability are different questions.

Myth

“Sigmoids are special.”
Nothing here needs the sigmoid in particular — any non-linear activation that can act like a step (or a hinge, for ReLU) works. The S-curve just made the step picture vivid.

Myth

“It means networks generalize.”
The construction fits the target on the region you tile — it's about representing a function, not predicting well on new inputs. Generalization is a separate story told by data and inductive bias.

Final takeaway. “Universal” means a single hidden layer of bumps can be arranged to match any continuous function as closely as you like. You didn't take that on faith—you flattened the sigmoids, glued the bumps, and stacked the towers yourself. The art of modern deep learning is doing the same thing efficiently: with depth, good optimizers, and the right inductive biases.
