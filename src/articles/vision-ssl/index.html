Vision Pretraining, Without Labels

Mask 75% of an image, predict the rest. Or pull augmentations of the same image together. Or distil a teacher that's itself an EMA of the student. Or predict a discrete tokenisation of patches. Four recipes, all label-free, all production. Live MAE demo + the table that tells you which to pick.

~20 min Computer Vision · Self-Supervised · Pretraining
Prelude

Why we don't train from scratch any more

ImageNet has 1.2M labelled images. Your sustainability lab has, optimistically, a few thousand labelled thermal frames or satellite tiles. A ViT-Base trained from scratch on that would overfit catastrophically. Self-supervised pretraining on the much larger pool of unlabelled images (millions, easy to scrape; billions, with a bit of ingenuity) gets you a backbone that needs only a thin fine-tune on labels.

The slogan. Spend your unlabelled-data calories on a self-supervised pretext task. Save your labelling budget for the part that actually needs labels (the head, sometimes a thin LoRA through the backbone).
Step 1

The MAE recipe — mask and reconstruct

Masked autoencoders (He et al., 2022) are the simplest recipe that works at scale. Take an image, divide into patches, randomly mask a large fraction (75% is the headline number), feed only the visible patches into a ViT encoder, and ask a small decoder to reconstruct the masked patches. Loss is mean-squared error in pixel space, only on the masked patches.

The asymmetry — an encoder that sees only 25% of the patches, a decoder that sees the full set with placeholders for the masked ones — is what makes this fast. Pretraining on ImageNet-1k takes under a day on 8 GPUs.

Step 2

The four families of SSL recipes

FamilyPretextExamplesWhat it learns wellWhat it struggles with
Contrastive Pull augmentations of same image together; push others apart SimCLR, MoCo, CLIP Discriminative features, transfer to classification Needs huge batches; weak on dense tasks
Distillation Match a teacher (often EMA of student) on different views DINO, DINOv2, iBOT Strong dense features; SOTA for downstream segmentation Stable training is delicate (centering, sharpening)
Generative Reconstruct masked pixels / tokens MAE, SimMIM, BEiT Sample efficiency; works on any modality Linear-probe accuracy lower than DINO/CLIP
Predictive Predict features of masked regions, not pixels I-JEPA, V-JEPA, V-JEPA 2 Very efficient; emphasis on semantic features Newer; tooling and recipes still maturing

A useful rule of thumb: for classification-flavoured tasks and small labelled fine-tune sets, prefer DINOv2 or CLIP. For dense prediction, segmentation, or any task where spatial detail matters, prefer DINOv2 or MAE. For unusual modalities (thermal, satellite, hyperspectral) MAE is the easiest to retrofit.

Step 4

What this looks like in your lab

  • Thermal pretraining. Stockpile every thermal frame from FLIR cameras on campus — unlabeled. Pretrain MAE on ~50k–200k frames. Fine-tune on a few hundred ApneaEye / JoulesEye labels. The backbone features will catch breathing-pattern motifs that a from-scratch model can't see.
  • Satellite pretraining. SentinelKilnDB has finite labelled tiles, but the source Sentinel-2 mosaic is essentially infinite. Pretrain MAE or DINOv2 on the unlabelled tiles; the kiln detector then trains on ~10× less labelled data for the same accuracy.
  • Domain-specific fine-tune of an off-the-shelf backbone. Even cheaper: take a public DINOv2 or CLIP backbone, do a short MAE-style continual pretrain on your in-domain unlabeled data, then fine-tune. The free big-pretrained backbone gives you a head-start; the domain pretrain shifts it toward your modality.
  • Modality embeddings for retrieval / VayuChat. CLIP-style alignment between (image, technical-text) pairs from your lab's papers / dashboards turns a free-text user question into a search over your dataset. Direct VayuChat / VayuBuddy upgrade.
Final takeaway. The hardest part of vision in a sustainability lab isn't training; it's labelling. SSL pretraining lets the unlabelled mountain do the heavy lifting and your annotators only touch the part that fundamentally needs human judgement.
