Vision Transformers, Step by Step

In 2020, Dosovitskiy et al. showed that a plain Transformer— the same architecture that had already eaten language— could match the best CNNs at image classification. The trick: treat an image like a sentence of little patches. This page walks through every mechanical step, with the pixels, vectors, and attention weights computed live on whatever image you pick.

~16 min Deep Learning · Vision · Transformers
Prelude

From CNNs to sentences of patches

A CNN processes an image by sliding small kernels over nearby pixels, layer after layer. The structure is hand-wired: locality, translation equivariance, a pyramid of scales. That wiring is what made CNNs dominate vision for a decade.

A Vision Transformer throws all of that out. It chops the image into non-overlapping patches, flattens each patch into a vector, and drops the resulting sequence into a vanilla Transformer—identical to one that reads English text. No convolutions. No pooling. Just self-attention.

This sounds reckless, and on small datasets it is: without CNN-style priors, ViTs underperform. But given enough training data, the Transformer learns spatial structure from scratch and then pulls ahead. By the end of this page you'll have executed every piece of the pipeline by hand on a real image.

Step 1

Chop the image into patches

The first thing a ViT does—really, the only image-specific thing it does—is split the input into a grid of equal patches. For a $64 \times 64$ image with $16 \times 16$ patches, you get a $4 \times 4 = 16$ patch grid. That grid is what becomes the "sentence" fed to the Transformer.

Drag the slider. Smaller patches mean more tokens (more compute, finer detail); larger patches mean fewer tokens (faster, but you lose spatial resolution). The original ViT-Base used 16×16 patches on 224×224 images, so 196 patch tokens per image.

Try the extremes. Set patch size to 32: four huge patches. Set patch size to 4: 256 tiny patches. The extreme ends are both useless— too coarse, or too many tokens to attend over. Somewhere in the middle is a sweet spot, and finding it per-problem is part of designing a ViT.
Step 2

Flatten each patch into a vector

A patch is a tiny image: $P \times P$ pixels, each with 3 RGB channels. To feed it into a Transformer, we just read out all $3P^2$ numbers in raster order and treat them as one vector:

Then we pass that vector through a single linear layer (the "patch embedding projection") to get a $D$-dimensional token. $D$ is typically 384, 768, or 1024 depending on the model size. After this step each patch looks just like a word embedding.

The projection is where ViT can "peek" at locality. The $D \times 3P^2$ projection matrix learns to weight pixels inside the patch. In practice it ends up computing gradient-like features—very similar to the first layer of a CNN! So ViT doesn't ignore pixel structure; it just lets the model learn the relevant filters instead of hard-coding them.
Step 3

Add position embeddings

Self-attention is permutation-invariant: swap two tokens and the output doesn't change. That's fine for a bag of words, fatal for an image where "top-left" and "bottom-right" mean very different things.

Fix: add a learned position embedding $p_i$ to each patch token. Every position in the grid gets its own vector, summed into the patch before attention sees it. Now tokens carry both content ("I'm a brown-furry patch") and location ("I'm at grid cell 3,2").

Toggle the effect

Below, you can strip position embeddings and watch attention collapse into a pure content-similarity search—identical patches attend to each other no matter where they are. Turn positions back on and attention regains spatial structure.

Step 4

Prepend the [CLS] token

We still need a single vector to read out a class label from. ViT borrows a trick from BERT: prepend a learnable "class" token to the sequence. The CLS token has no pixels behind it—it's just a free parameter vector that the model gets to optimize.

Because self-attention lets every token look at every other, the CLS token can "query" all the patches, gathering information from the whole image. After the Transformer block, its final embedding is fed into a simple MLP that outputs the class logits.

Why a special token instead of averaging patches? Averaging is fine, and in fact many newer models (DeiT, MAE variants) do exactly that. But a learnable CLS gives the model a place it gets to choose how to aggregate—different heads may grab different patches for different parts of the label. Think of it as a learned, attention-weighted average, shaped specifically for classification.
Step 5

Self-attention: patches listen to patches

Here's the heart of ViT. Every token projects itself into a query, a key, and a value. The attention weight from token $i$ to token $j$ is a softmax of the scaled dot product $q_i \cdot k_j$, and each token's new value is a weighted sum of all the other tokens' values.

Step 6

Putting it all together

One "encoder block" of a ViT is: self-attention → residual add → LayerNorm → MLP → residual add → LayerNorm. ViT stacks 12, 24, or 32 of these blocks, then takes the final CLS token, passes it through a one-hidden-layer MLP, and reads out classification logits. The whole recipe:

Step 7

Four things people get wrong about ViTs

Myth

"ViTs have no inductive bias for images."
They have less than a CNN, but far from none. The patch embedding is a convolution with stride-$P$ kernel. Position embeddings encode a grid. And self-attention itself is a smooth, differentiable prior against gibberish permutations.

Myth

"They need hundreds of millions of images."
The original ViT did, because of its weak inductive bias. Post-2021 variants (DeiT, Swin, MAE pre-training) train on ImageNet-1k alone and match or beat CNNs. Data hunger was a recipe choice, not a fundamental limit.

Myth

"Attention weights tell you what the model sees."
Tempting, but noisy. Attention is one of many signals in a deep residual network. The same prediction can be supported by many different attention patterns, and gradient-based attributions often disagree with attention. Treat heatmaps as suggestive, not evidence.

Myth

"Smaller patches are always better."
Halving the patch size quadruples the token count and quadruples squared the attention cost ($N^2$). Below some point, you pay 16× the compute for a marginal accuracy bump. Hybrid models (Swin, MaxViT) use a local attention window to buy both without the quadratic cost.

Final takeaway. A Vision Transformer is a bag of patches plus their positions, poured through the exact same attention block you'd use on text. You've now seen every number along the way: the patch grid, the flattened vectors, the position tags, the CLS token, and the attention heatmap. The thing that felt like a magic trick is really a dot product, a softmax, and a weighted sum—applied to pieces of a picture instead of words of a sentence.
